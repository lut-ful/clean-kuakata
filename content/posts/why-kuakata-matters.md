# Why Kuakata Matters

Kuakata — locally known as **"Shagor Konna"** (Daughter of the Sea) — is one of the rarest beaches on Earth.

It is the only place in Bangladesh where visitors can watch both the **sunrise and the sunset** from the same shore, over the open expanse of the Bay of Bengal. This is not a marketing claim. It is a geographical fact that makes Kuakata genuinely unique in the world.

## What Makes Kuakata Different

Kuakata's 18-kilometre coastline sits at the southernmost tip of the Patuakhali district, where the land meets the Bay of Bengal in a wide, sweeping arc. The beach faces south — meaning both the eastern morning sun and the western evening sun are visible over water.

The surrounding ecosystem amplifies its value:

- **Gangamati Reserved Forest** to the east — home to mangroves that store more carbon per hectare than tropical rainforests
- **Fatra Reserved Forest** to the west — an expanse of coastal woodland and tidal creeks
- **Lal Kakrar Char** — a sandbar accessible by boat where thousands of red crabs gather in season
- **Irrawaddy dolphins** that move through the shallow coastal waters
- Migratory birds following the **East Asian–Australasian Flyway**, stopping on their journeys between Siberia and Australia

## The Crisis

But today, Kuakata is in trouble.

Eight of its original eighteen kilometres of beach have been lost to coastal erosion — accelerated by plastic pollution that damages the mangrove root systems that naturally buffer the shore. Ghost fishing nets tangle beneath the surface. Polythene bags coat the sand at high tide. Tourist numbers are falling. Fishermen report declining catches.

The story of Kuakata is the story of Bangladesh's relationship with its coast: a resource of extraordinary natural value, neglected by infrastructure and overwhelmed by waste.

## Why We Act

CleanKuakata exists because Kuakata is worth saving — and because saving it is possible.

A single coordinated clean-up on June 5, 2026 — World Environment Day — will not solve a structural problem. But it will demonstrate what organised collective action can achieve. It will generate data. It will create pressure for policy change. It will build a community of people who care.

And it will give Kuakata a day that belongs to its future, not just its past.

**That is worth showing up for.**
